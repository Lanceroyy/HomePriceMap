About Home Price Map

Home Price Map is a free, independent tool for understanding what housing actually costs across the United States — not as a single national headline number, but county by county and city by city, the way the housing market really works. It started from a simple frustration: if you want to compare home prices between the place you live and a place you're thinking about moving to, most sources either bury the answer in raw spreadsheets or only show you one listing at a time. This site turns that data into a single interactive map you can actually read at a glance.

What the site covers

Every U.S. county and more than 17,000 cities and towns are plotted on two interactive maps, color-coded by median home value. Click any area and you'll see its current typical home value, how that value has changed over the past year, its local violent and property crime rates where reporting data exists, and — for counties — how home prices compare to what people in that area actually earn. That last figure, the price-to-income ratio, is the one most cost-of-living tools leave out, and it's often the most honest answer to the real question behind a move: not just "what do homes cost here," but "could someone with a normal local job actually afford one."

Why it's built the way it is

The whole site is static and automated. A scheduled job checks for new data every day and rebuilds the maps and the thousands of individual location pages without any manual work, which is how the numbers stay current with each monthly data release. There are no accounts, no paywalls, and no usage limits — the goal is for anyone to be able to look something up in a few seconds and leave with a clear answer.

Who makes it

Home Price Map is a solo, independent project, not a real estate brokerage, lender, or data reseller. It doesn't list homes for sale, generate leads, or represent any agent or company. It exists to make public housing data easier to explore, and it's supported by advertising and relevant affiliate links rather than by charging visitors or selling their information.

A note on the numbers

All of the data here comes from public sources — Zillow Research for home values, the FBI for crime, and the U.S. Census Bureau for income and geography. None of it is proprietary, and none of it is a substitute for professional advice when you're making an actual purchase. Every figure is an estimate with its own limitations, which are explained in detail on the Data & Methodology page. The aim is to be useful and honest about what the numbers can and can't tell you, rather than to imply more precision than the underlying data supports.
